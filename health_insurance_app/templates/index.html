Project Description

Using county-level health insurance data from 2008 to 2014 gathered by the US Census,
I created a model to predict whether a county would be above the national
average in percentage of population that has health insurance.

Model

After trying out many classifiers, I decided to go with logistic regression
which consistently had the best results. The only input feature I used was
the percentage of the county's population at or below 138% poverty level.
While modeling, I tried to incorporate political data, but I only had gathered
the necessary data for 2012 and 2014. Though this did improve the model's results
for these years, I decided to leave this feature out until I can get more data to
cover a broader time period. That said, due to the high correlation between poverty
level and the percentage of population with health insurance, the model was able to
get decent results:

  • 2008: ROC AUC Score - .73, Accuracy - .81
  • 2009: ROC AUC Score - .76, Accuracy - .86
  • 2010: ROC AUC Score - .74, Accuracy - .82
  • 2011: ROC AUC Score - .76, Accuracy - .84
  • 2012: ROC AUC Score - .75, Accuracy - .83
  • 2013: ROC AUC Score - .75, Accuracy - .83
  • 2014: ROC AUC Score - .71, Accuracy - .78

The lowest score is in 2014. I think this might be due to the fact that the correlation
between poverty and lack of health insurance is weaker as more people are getting
health insurance under the Affordable Care Act.
